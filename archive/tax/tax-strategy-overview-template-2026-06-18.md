## 2. Key Tax Concepts for This Family

### 2.1 Grantor Trust vs. Non-Grantor Trust

**Plain language:** A "grantor trust" is a trust where, for tax purposes, the IRS treats the assets as if the founder (grantor) still owns them — even if the trust legally holds title. This means the grantor pays income tax on the trust's earnings, and in many cases the trust's assets receive a stepped-up cost basis when the grantor dies.

**A non-grantor trust** is taxed as its own separate entity, pays its own taxes at trust tax rates (which reach the top 37% bracket at just ~$15,200 of income in 2026), and issues a Schedule K-1 to beneficiaries for distributed income.

**Critical distinction (IRS Rev. Ruling 2023-2, 2023):**
- **Revocable (living) trusts** → assets remain in the grantor's estate → **DO receive a step-up in cost basis at death** (IRC § 1014)
- **Irrevocable grantor trusts** where assets are outside the estate → **DO NOT receive a step-up in cost basis at death** per IRS Rev. Rul. 2023-2

This is one of the most significant planning considerations for assets that have appreciated substantially. An irrevocable trust holding $1M of stock purchased at $100K will owe capital gains tax on $900K of gain if sold after the grantor's death — whereas the same stock in a revocable trust would have its basis "stepped up" to $1M at death, eliminating that gain entirely.

### 2.2 Step-Up in Basis

**Plain language:** "Basis" is what you paid for something. "Stepped-up basis" means: when you inherit an asset, the IRS treats your basis as the fair market value on the date the person died — not what they originally paid. This eliminates decades of accumulated capital gains.

**Example:** Grandfather bought stock for $50,000 in 1985. It's worth $2,000,000 today. If he sells it, he owes capital gains tax on $1,950,000. If he dies holding it in a revocable trust, the heirs inherit it with a basis of $2,000,000 — and owe zero capital gains if they sell immediately.

**2026 context:** The federal estate and gift tax exemption is $15,000,000 per person ($30,000,000 per married couple) as of 2026. For families below this threshold, the step-up in basis is often the most valuable estate tax benefit available.

**QTIP double step-up:** Married couples can potentially achieve a "double step-up" — once when the first spouse dies, again when the surviving spouse dies — using a Qualified Terminable Interest Property (QTIP) trust structure. *Consult estate planning counsel.*

### 2.3 Schedule K-1

**Plain language:** If the family owns interests in a partnership, LLC taxed as a partnership, S corporation, trust, or estate, you receive a Schedule K-1 each year. It reports your share of that entity's income, deductions, and credits, which you then report on your personal tax return. K-1s frequently arrive late (March–April), and extensions are often needed as a result.

**Filing implication:** IRS instructions state: "The partnership uses Schedule K-1 to report your share of the partnership's income, deductions, credits, etc. Keep it for your records." K-1s must be matched to the correct entity and taxpayer before filing.

### 2.4 Capital Gains: Short-Term vs. Long-Term

**Plain language:** If you sell an investment you've held for more than one year, any profit is taxed at the "long-term capital gains" rate (0%, 15%, or 20% depending on income). If you sell within one year, the profit is taxed as ordinary income — potentially at 37%. Holding period is one of the simplest and most powerful tax levers available.

### 2.5 Tax-Loss Harvesting

**Plain language:** If some investments have lost value, selling them deliberately generates a "loss" that can offset gains elsewhere — reducing the tax bill. The key rule: you cannot buy back a "substantially identical" security within 30 days (the "wash sale" rule). This strategy is most valuable in years with significant realized gains.

### 2.6 Charitable Giving: Stock vs. Cash

**Plain language:** Donating appreciated stock directly to a Donor-Advised Fund (DAF) or charity is almost always better than selling the stock and donating cash. Why: when you donate stock, you (a) avoid the capital gains tax on the appreciation, and (b) get a charitable deduction for the full fair market value. Donating cash loses the first benefit entirely.

**Example:** You own $100,000 of stock with a $10,000 basis. Scenario A — sell it, pay $13,500 in capital gains tax (15%), donate $86,500 cash → deduction of $86,500. Scenario B — donate the stock directly → deduction of $100,000, zero capital gains tax owed. Scenario B saves $13,500 in tax plus yields a $13,500 larger deduction.

## 4. Document Collection Requirements

Per IRS guidance, records must support every item of income, deduction, or credit on every return filed. Required documents include:

**Investment accounts:** Form 1099-DIV (dividends), Form 1099-INT (interest), Form 1099-B (sales proceeds and cost basis), Schedule K-1s from partnerships/funds

**Real property:** Closing statements (purchase and sale), records of capital improvements (add to cost basis), depreciation schedules, rental income records, mortgage interest statements (Form 1098)

**Trust entities:** Trust agreements (to confirm grantor/non-grantor status), Form 1041 filings, beneficiary distribution records, Schedule K-1s issued to beneficiaries

**Charitable giving:** Contemporaneous written acknowledgment from the charity for any gift ≥ $250 (required by IRS); Form 8283 for non-cash gifts; DAF grant confirmations

**Retention rules (IRS event-driven, not flat):**
- General returns: until the applicable statute of limitations expires (typically 3 years from filing date)
- Returns with substantial understatement: 6 years
- No return filed: indefinitely
- Property records: until limitations expire for the **disposal year** (not the acquisition year)
- Employment tax records: at least 4 years
- Worthless securities / bad debt: 7 years

*Source: IRS Publication 583; IRS recordkeeping guidance; 26 U.S.C. § 6001*

## 6. Tax-Sensitive Transaction Checklist

Before executing any of the following, confirm the tax impact with the CPA:

- Selling any appreciated security (capital gains trigger)
- Selling real property (depreciation recapture, capital gains)
- Converting traditional IRA to Roth IRA (full conversion amount is ordinary income)
- Taking a trust distribution (may shift tax liability to beneficiary via K-1)
- Making a large charitable gift (verify AGI deductibility limits: generally 60% of AGI for cash to public charity, 30% for appreciated property)
- Establishing or modifying any trust (grantor trust status, estate inclusion analysis)
- Gifting assets to family members (annual exclusion: $19,000/recipient in 2026; lifetime exemption: $15,000,000)

## Narrative for Future Generations

*This section is S1 — visible to all family members.*

The family's approach to taxes has always been shaped by a simple belief: we should pay what we legally owe, not a dollar more — and every dollar kept in the family is a dollar that compounds for the generations who come after us. This is not about avoidance; it is about stewardship.

The decisions encoded in our trust structures, our choice of which accounts hold which assets, and the timing of our sales and gifts are not accidental. They reflect decades of planning. A future steward reading this record should understand not just the current structure, but the reasoning behind it — so that when the tax laws change (and they always do), the family can adapt its strategy without losing the underlying intention.

The most important tax principle for this family: **the structure of ownership matters as much as the assets themselves.** How an asset is held — in whose name, in what type of trust, in which state — determines its tax treatment for decades. Change the structure only deliberately, with counsel, and with a clear-eyed understanding of what you are giving up.
